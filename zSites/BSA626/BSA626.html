Alright, here we are day 3 of the project. Pitch completed and have the greenlight to go ahead with the project. There have been a few thoughts overnight about some shots which I think could be cool for both technical learning opportunities and just think they would look cool. E.g. The opening shot is dark, spotlight clicks on as the character is blinded by the light for a little bit (first time reveal of the character to the audience) then lights flicker on behind the character like the scene in batman when the batcave lights turn on.

Will pitch the idea today to the classmates.

The approach I am going to try for organising the different stages of my project, I will start with the end goal in mind and build my schedule from there.

End goal :

“Create and complete a high quality cinematic for a game which I can feel proud of for a showreel piece”.

  1. Research
  2. Create a pitch for a storyline which fits the game.
  3. Research how I am going to achieve this, and what are some problems I might expect to run into (from both external and personal limitations)
  4. Experiment at this stage with potential problems and find solutions to overcome them
  5. Write a strong script for what exactly is going to happen in the cinematic
  6. Visually mock up a storyboard for how the look and feel is going to be in the final product with color, movement, feel, emotion, flow and pace, character, etc…
  7. Review at this stage, make changes as required. Refine the idea and solidify it.
  8. Plan with SMART goals in mind and divy out tasks and time limits
  9. GANTT CHARTS!
  10. Start Development.

Introduction


For this project I will build a high quality intro cinematic for a game called "bashful".
Clean and focused on the character and what he does.

The mechanics of the game is to "push the characters buttons" via clicking the character enough to make him/her/it so annoyed that he/she/it will burst out in anger to start destroying everything and anything in its way.

This game idea was inspired by two things , the music video "The Verve - Bitter Sweet Symphony" and looking at the Elmo puppet in the classroom.

Research & Development

The early stages of this project brought up a few very important questions. For example:

"What makes something cute?"

A study by Konrad Lorenz in the early 1940s found that the shape of an infant's head positively correlated with adult caregiving and an increased perception of "cute". ... The study concluded that a large head shape increased perceived cuteness, which then elicited a positive response in adult caretaking.

  • Change of head proportions (especially the relative size of the maxilla and mandible) as a function of age
  • Use of eyebrows is an effective way of making puppy dog eyes
